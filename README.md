## Findings

### 1. Demographic Profile

The dataset reflects a highly educated and young electorate. A significant majority of respondents are male (approximately 75% in the primary sample) and fall within the 18–35 years age bracket. Education levels are notably high, with most participants holding an HND/B.A./BSc. degree or a Master’s/Postgraduate Diploma.

### 2. Platform Prevalence

Facebook and WhatsApp emerged as the primary hubs for political misinformation. Analysis shows that 34% of respondents encountered fake news on WhatsApp, followed by 28% on Facebook. WhatsApp’s interpersonal and encrypted nature makes it a dominant source for the circulation of election-related falsehoods.

### 3. Perceptual Gap (Third-Person Effect)

The data shows a clear divergence between awareness and perceived personal impact. While respondents report high levels of _Awareness of Fake News_ ($M \approx 3.65$) and _Inoculation_ ($M \approx 3.92$), they report significantly lower _Negative Influence_ on themselves ($M \approx 2.45$). This confirms the Third-Person Effect, where voters believe they are immune to misinformation that they perceive as a threat to others.

### 4. Correlation and Reliability

Spearman’s Rho was utilized because the data violated normality assumptions. The study found that while awareness was high, its ability to prevent the influence of fake news was limited, particularly when misinformation aligned with a user's partisan identity. The reliability of the final 11-item instrument was high, with Cronbach’s Alpha ($\alpha$) values ranging between 0.712 and 0.938.

---

## Summary

The statistical analysis reveals an _epistemic overconfidence_ within the Nigerian information ecosystem. Voters are acutely aware of the _fake news_ era and believe they possess the psychological inoculation required to resist it. However, the prevalence of misinformation on WhatsApp and Facebook remains high, driven by social motivations like altruism and civil obligation.

Despite high self-reported awareness, the persistence of misinformation suggests that subjective literacy (self-confidence) does not always equate to objective discernment, as partisan labels often override cognitive resistance.

Strategies for future elections should focus on moving from general awareness to specific technical discernment skills, such as identifying impersonation and sensationalism.
